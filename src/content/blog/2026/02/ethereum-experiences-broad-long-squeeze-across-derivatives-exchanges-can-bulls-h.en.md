## Overview

Ethereum has faced significant selling pressure, resulting in a sharp decline since last Friday. The price has dropped markedly, losing a crucial psychological level, which has prompted a reassessment of market sentiment and risk appetite.

## What Happened?

Ethereum has experienced a dramatic 28% decline following the loss of the $3,000 psychological support level. Initially, the pullback seemed controlled, but it quickly escalated into one of the most aggressive downside moves in recent months. This shift reflects a sudden change in market sentiment across the cryptocurrency space.

On January 31st, Ethereum underwent a major capitulation event, with the price collapsing from above $3,000 to around $2,350 within a few hours. This significant single-day correction indicates forced selling rather than a systematic distribution of assets. As the price plummeted, a dense cluster of stop-loss orders and liquidations was triggered, creating a surge of downside momentum and overwhelming bid-side liquidity. This rapid decline erased weeks of bullish positioning for traders who anticipated a continuation above $3,000, leading to a broad reset in sentiment and derivatives exposure.

The psychological impact of losing such a widely watched level intensified the sell-off, reinforcing risk-off behavior across both spot and futures markets. With Ethereum now stabilizing below previous support, investors are left to ponder whether this move signals a temporary washout or the onset of a deeper corrective phase. The next few sessions will be critical for determining if demand can resurface following this violent reset.

## Market-Wide Deleveraging Resets Ethereum’s Derivatives Landscape

According to an analyst from CryptoQuant, recent on-chain data indicates that the Ethereum sell-off was driven by a market-wide leverage flush rather than organic spot distribution. The total liquidated long positions surged to approximately $485 million, marking one of the largest liquidation events since earlier in the cycle. This surge forces a reset of the derivatives market by unwinding over-leveraged positions that had built up over time.

However, a closer examination reveals a significant divergence in liquidation data among exchanges. For instance, Binance recorded only around $40 million in long liquidations during this period, accounting for less than 10% of total global liquidations. This suggests that traders on Binance were either less overextended or employed stricter risk management strategies, allowing them to withstand the rapid downside move more effectively. In contrast, other platforms faced severe liquidation cascades, indicating that excessive leverage was concentrated elsewhere.

This type of long squeeze, while painful for bullish positions, often purges speculative excess and can set the stage for market stabilization as traders seek a new equilibrium. It is essential to monitor open interest and funding rates outside of Binance to understand the core drivers of volatility that originated beyond its ecosystem.
